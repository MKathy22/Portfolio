Ludwig Maximilian University Munich

Connecting Newcomers
From Day One.

A mobile app giving LMU's exchange programme a digital backbone — connecting international students with experienced buddies before they even arrive.

UX Design LMU Munich Individual Project
Problem Goal Solution Research Users Prototyping Features Process Accessibility Future Reflection
Personal Motivation

This project is personal — I've lived both sides of this program

The LMU Buddy Program pairs incoming exchange students with experienced LMU students. Buddies help them navigate university life, settle into Munich, build connections, and find their footing in a new environment.

I've been part of this program on both sides — and seen exactly where its good intentions break down. This project is a direct response to both experiences.

2×

Exchange Student

Two semesters abroad — experiencing firsthand what it means to arrive somewhere new without enough support.

4×

LMU Buddy

Four semesters as a buddy — motivated to help, but lacking the tools to do it well.

The Problem

The program has the right intent — but neither side has the tools to make it work

The program relies entirely on informal coordination — no platform, no shared space, no structure. It works when individuals fill the gap themselves. Most of the time, they don't.

01

No dedicated platform for either side

Buddies and exchange students rely on WhatsApp and email — no single place to coordinate or find program information.

02

No structure for the buddy relationship

Most pairings fade after the first message. Buddies have no way to track contact or know if their support is landing.

03

No community or shared events space

Events and social opportunities exist but are impossible to discover. There's no community layer connecting exchange students to each other or to the buddy network.

The Goal

Design a dedicated mobile experience that activates the LMU Buddy Program — giving exchange students and their buddies the structure, tools, and motivation to build a meaningful connection from the moment they arrive.

The Solution

A structured companion app — built around the exchange student experience.

LMU Buddy gives the program a digital backbone: onboarding, matching, communication, and event discovery — in one university-branded app.

01

Smart buddy matching

Matched by faculty, home country, and language — designed for the exchange student context.

02

Structured onboarding flow

Guided first steps prompt both sides to connect and schedule their first meeting before the semester gets busy.

03

In-app messaging & resources

One place for chat, shared documents, and practical info — no more scattered channels.

04

Event discovery & RSVP

Events surfaced proactively, with easy registration for students new to the city.

Research & Insights

The problem was real — and well-documented. But the existing experience wasn't designed at all.

Exchange students

face a compressed transition — housing, registration, language, and social isolation all within the first 2–3 weeks

Buddy programs work —

research shows peer mentorship accelerates integration and reduces isolation in the first weeks abroad

But only if activated

LMU buddy pairings often "fade out" within 2 weeks — not from lack of interest, but from lack of structure

Practical barriers dominate

Bureaucracy, accommodation, and navigating a new city were the highest-stress tasks — exactly where a buddy could help

Buddy volunteers

are motivated to help but lack tools to stay in touch — good intentions that fade once their own semester picks up

Current digital touchpoints

are scattered across the LMU website, email newsletters, and Facebook groups — no single entry point

Research Method Note

Research combined a competitive audit of existing buddy and peer-mentorship apps, a content review of the current LMU Buddy Program website, and qualitative interviews with LMU students who had participated as buddies or as incoming exchange students. These conversations surfaced the "fade-out" pattern as the central design problem — and directly shaped the onboarding flow and nudge system in the solution.

Target Users

Two distinct users — one shared semester

The app serves both sides of the pairing. Needs diverge sharply in the first weeks and converge as the semester progresses.

Exchange Students

Arriving for one or two semesters. Need practical info, a reliable contact, and orientation before their first lecture.

LMU Buddy Volunteers

Motivated LMU students who signed up to help. Need tools that make staying in touch feel easy, not like admin work.

High Language Diversity

Exchange students arrive from many countries. Language accessibility and culturally neutral design were non-negotiable throughout.

Mobile-First Context

A dedicated app — not a web portal — fits how students look for help during orientation.

Low Friction Tolerance

Both users operate at high cognitive load early on. Every feature had to justify its presence — if it added steps without value, it was cut.

Problem Statement

The hardest part was designing for a relationship that needs to form before it can function

Exchange students need support most in their first 2–3 weeks. But this is also when the buddy relationship is newest and most fragile.

01

The structural gap

The design problem wasn't "build a new program" — it was "give the existing one the backbone it's missing."

02

The dual-user challenge

The solution needed to work for two users with different mental models. Designing only for exchange students would have produced a half-solution.

03

Scope constraint

Ruthless scoping was a design decision: better to solve one problem well than sketch ten solutions shallowly.

Prototyping

Sketch, Build, Test, Repeat

Four rounds of iteration — each one answering a specific question about user behaviour or interface logic

Wireframes & Low Fidelity

The process started with paper sketches to rapidly explore layout ideas without getting attached to visual details. These were translated into lo-fi wireframes in Figma — focusing purely on structure, user flow, and information hierarchy for both user roles.

  • Sketched key screens: onboarding, matching, buddy space, events
  • Mapped full app flow for both exchange student and buddy
  • Identified structural gaps before investing in visual design
  • Used lo-fi for first round of task-based walkthroughs

Mid Fidelity & Usability Testing

Mid-fi screens added realistic content and basic visual structure, making the prototype testable with real users. Structured usability tests uncovered four critical issues that were all resolved before moving to high fidelity.

  • Matching flow felt opaque — users didn't understand the criteria
  • "First meeting" notification prompt was confusing
  • Resource library buried two levels too deep
  • Profile setup felt like a form, not an onboarding

High Fidelity

The hi-fi prototype applied LMU's brand system — typography, colour, and iconography — to produce a polished, interactive MVP. Expert feedback and a heuristic evaluation led to final refinements in onboarding progress visibility, error recovery, and the addition of a dedicated buddy onboarding track.

  • Full visual design aligned to LMU brand guidelines
  • Buddy onboarding track added after expert review
  • System status and progress visibility improved
  • Interactive MVP ready for presentation and handoff
Key Features

The final MVP — scoped to what matters most

Login & Registration

A secure, role-aware entry point

  • Separate flows for exchange students and buddies
  • University email verification for LMU affiliation
  • Password reset and session persistence
Onboarding & Matching

A first experience that earns trust immediately

  • Role-based onboarding (exchange student vs. buddy)
  • Profile built progressively, not as a gatekeeper
  • Matching shows rationale — faculty, language, home country
Buddy Connection

Structured relationship tools, not just a chat window

  • Shared space for messages, resources & notes
  • First-meeting prompt with conversation starters
  • Progress nudges to keep the relationship alive
Events & Program Info

The right information, at the right moment

  • Personalised feed: orientation, campus tours, social events
  • One-tap RSVP with buddy coordination
  • Curated resource library replacing scattered PDFs
Admin Dashboard

Visibility for the people running the program

  • Overview of all active pairings and their status
  • Flag quiet relationships for follow-up
  • Event management and tracking

Accessibility

Designed to work for everyone — regardless of language, ability, or device

01

Language & readability

Content available in English and German. Plain language throughout — short instructions for users navigating in a second language.

02

Contrast & visual hierarchy

Colour combinations meet WCAG AA standards. Typography scales with system font size and touch targets meet minimum size requirements.

03

Inclusive onboarding

Each onboarding step is self-contained, progress is always visible, and users can skip or revisit without losing their place.

Future Development

If this project continued, these would be the next priorities

01

Admin dashboard

A coordinator view for LMU staff — with visibility into pairing status and the ability to intervene when relationships go quiet.

02

Group buddy sessions

Group matching for cohorts from the same home university — reducing isolation and scaling beyond one-to-one pairings.

03

LMU portal integration

Single sign-on with LMU's student portal — exchange students already have university credentials and shouldn't need a separate account.

04

Mid-semester check-in

A structured nudge at the 4-week mark — where most relationships fade — with prompts to sustain engagement through the semester.

Reflection

What I'd do differently

Test with actual exchange students earlier

Participants were primarily domestic students. Real exchange students would have surfaced language and cultural friction much sooner.

Design the buddy side in parallel

I over-prioritised the exchange student perspective early on. Running buddy-side tests in parallel from the start would have produced a more balanced solution.

Design for the 4-week drop-off

The MVP solves activation well. Sustaining relationships past the first month remains the key open design question.

Key Takeaway
The most important UX decision wasn't a screen or a flow — it was choosing to design for both sides of the relationship, not just the one asking for help.
